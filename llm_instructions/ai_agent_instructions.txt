SOURCING & CITATIONS
======================================

Step 1: Consult Verified Wiki Articles (Preferred Source)

Before writing, you should consult the wiki's existing verified articles. These are marked with {{Article quality|verified}}, which outputs an invisible marker (a hidden span with `data-quality="verified"`) that you can detect in the page source but readers don't see. These verified articles represent the "golden set" of editor-approved content and establish the project's conventions and terminology.

Use verified wiki articles to:
- Define core concepts, people, and events specific to the project
- Ensure consistent internal linking (e.g., link to [[Remilia Corporation]], [[Milady Maker]] if they exist)
- Maintain consistent terminology (e.g., use "Network spirituality" as defined in existing articles)
- Follow established formatting patterns and section structures

Key verified articles to reference for conventions:
- NFT collections: See how Milady Maker, Remilio Babies are structured
- Organizations: See Remilia Corporation for parent_group usage
- People: Note pseudonymous vs. legal identity handling
- Concepts: See how coined terms are attributed

Clarification on Revision:
While verified articles represent established consensus, factual claims are still subject to revision. If your task is to revise, update, or expand an existing article, you MUST use your web search tools to find new, reliable sources to support changes, even if they contradict existing text. The goal is progressive improvement with the best available sourcing.

Step 2: Use Web Search (When Needed)

You MUST use your web search tools to find reliable sources for:
- Direct claims about the specific subject (names, dates, events, actions)
- Direct quotations
- Controversial or surprising claims
- Specific statistics, numbers, or data points
- Topics not covered in the local knowledge base

You may write general background information from your knowledge WITHOUT searching, including:
- Common knowledge explanations (e.g., "NFTs are digital tokens recorded on blockchains")
- Uncontroversial context (e.g., "online communities often develop distinctive aesthetics")
- General technical or conceptual background (e.g., "eating disorders can have competitive dynamics")
- Established historical context (e.g., "the early internet fostered pseudonymous communities")

UNRELIABLE SOURCES - DO NOT CITE:
- NEVER cite IQ.wiki (iq.wiki) - unreliable, often inaccurate, user-generated content
- NEVER cite Know Your Meme (knowyourmeme.com) - tertiary source with quality issues
- NEVER cite NFT Price Floor - bad quality content and inaccurate details
- These sources may appear in search results but must not be used as citations
- If these are the only sources available, search for better alternatives or note the lack of reliable sourcing

CRITICAL SOURCING GUIDANCE FOR SUBCULTURAL TOPICS:
- This is a subcultural wiki, not an academic journal. Excessive academic citations are a telltale sign of AI writing.
- Prefer primary sources (websites, tweets, blog posts, interviews, news articles) over academic journals when documenting subcultural phenomena.
- Use academic sources sparingly and only when they are necessary to support claims of equal academic nature.
- AVOID the pattern of citing 5+ academic journals in a single article about internet culture - this is unnatural and overly formal.
- When academic knowledge informs your understanding, incorporate it into your prose without necessarily citing every academic paper on the broader topic.

Step 3: Cite Key Claims Judiciously

Named References (Avoiding Duplicate Citations):

When citing the same source multiple times, use MediaWiki's named reference system:

First use - full citation with name attribute:
<ref name="shortname">{{Cite web|author=...|title=...|url=...}}</ref>

Subsequent uses - empty tag with same name:
<ref name="shortname" />

Example:
First use: The essay argues X.<ref name="fang2023">{{Cite web|author=Charlotte Fang|date=March 31, 2023|title=Alignment Fraud|url=https://...}}</ref>
Later: Fang also states Y.<ref name="fang2023" />

Name conventions:
- Use descriptive short names: author+year format (e.g., "fang2023", "johnson2022")
- For multiple works by same author in same year, add suffix (e.g., "fang2023a", "fang2023b")
- Keep names lowercase and simple (no spaces or special characters)

NEVER duplicate full citations - always use named references for repeated sources.

DO NOT CITE:
- Common knowledge (e.g., "Ethereum is a blockchain platform")
- General background context (e.g., "profile picture collections became popular in 2021")
- Uncontroversial explanations of concepts (e.g., "DAOs use token-based governance")
- Widely understood technical details (e.g., "smart contracts execute automatically")
- Non-specific contextual statements (e.g., "digital art movements emerged in the 2010s")

DO CITE:
1. Direct claims about the specific subject (e.g., "Milady Maker launched in August 2021")
2. Direct quotations (e.g., Charlotte Fang's statements)
3. Controversial or surprising claims (e.g., "the collection sold out in minutes")
4. Specific statistics or data points (e.g., "the floor price reached 5 ETH")
5. Claims about reception, impact, or significance (e.g., "critics praised the aesthetic")

This approach creates natural, readable prose while ensuring verifiability for claims that matter.

Inside the <ref> tags, use appropriate citation templates (e.g., {{Cite web}}, {{Cite news}}, {{Cite tweet}}).
This structure is essential for compatibility with the Citoid extension, as detailed in remiliawiki_extension_instructions.txt.

When using citation templates:
- Fill in all available information (url, title, author, date, etc.)
- LEAVE archive fields BLANK (archive-url, archive-is, archive-date, archive-is-date, screenshot)
- These archive fields are for manual completion later

DO NOT fabricate or hallucinate sources.
Ensure all links, DOIs, and ISBNs are valid.
NEVER cite unreliable sources including IQ.wiki or Know Your Meme.

======================================
ARTICLE WRITING WORKFLOW
======================================

When asked to write an article, follow this workflow:

1. Read template_reference.txt to know what templates are available
2. Read category_reference.txt to know what categories to use
3. Consult local knowledge base (article_*.txt files) for related concepts and terminology
4. Review wikipedia_manual_of_style.txt and ai_writing_pitfalls.txt for style guidance
5. Search the web for reliable sources to support key factual claims about the subject
6. Write the article in wikitext using appropriate templates
7. Ensure every article has:
   - Line 1: {{SHORTDESC:...}}
   - Line 2: {{Article quality|unverified}}
   - Line 3: (blank line)
   - Line 4: Article body begins with '''bold title'''
   - Appropriate infobox (if applicable) - place after short description, can be before or after article quality banner
   - Well-structured prose with judicious inline citations (typically 2-5 citations for a short article, not 10+)
   - ==References== section with {{Reflist}}
   - Typically 2-4 [[Category:...]] tags, preferring those in category_reference.txt

Citation Target: Aim for 2-5 citations in a typical short article (2-4 paragraphs), focusing on the most important factual claims. Longer articles may have more, but avoid the pattern of citing every sentence. Let general context breathe without citations.

======================================
AI-GENERATED CONTENT MARKING
======================================

MANDATORY: Every article you generate MUST include {{Article quality|unverified}} as the SECOND LINE, immediately after {{SHORTDESC:...}}.

CRITICAL ORDERING - First 4 lines of every article:
Line 1: {{SHORTDESC:...}}
Line 2: {{Article quality|unverified}}
Line 3: (blank line)
Line 4: '''Article title''' is the opening paragraph...

This serves two critical purposes:
1. Warns readers that AI may have hallucinated or misrepresented facts
2. Helps editors identify which articles need human fact-checking

Only human editors may change this template status during revision and fact-checking.
Never generate articles with {{Article quality|wip}} or {{Article quality|verified}} - only humans can change status.
The three statuses are: unverified (AI-generated), wip (under human revision), verified (fully fact-checked).
